The engine hummed a low, dying note as the red warning lights bathed the cockpit in an eerie glow. It wasn't the silence that scared him, but the sudden vibration that rattled his teeth. He gripped the controls, knuckles white, knowing that the landing gear had been sheared off in the asteroid field miles back.

"Hold on," he whispered to no one in particular, his voice barely audible over the screaming atmospheric entry. The planet below was a swirl of brown and green, rushing up to meet him faster than his calculations had predicted.

The console sparked, spitting a shower of hot embers onto his flight suit. He slapped them away instinctively, his eyes never leaving the altimeter. Three thousand feet. Too fast. He was coming in way too fast. He toggled the thruster sequence, praying the auxiliary fuel lines were still intact.

Thud. A dull impact reverberated through the hull. Then another. The ship groaned, metal twisting under stress it wasn't designed for. He squeezed his eyes shut as the ground filled the viewport, bracing for the inevitable crash that would either end his journey or begin a much harder one.

Silence followed. Not the peaceful kind, but the ringing silence of shock. Dust motes danced in the shafts of sunlight piercing through the rent hull. He unbuckled, his hands shaking, and took his first breath of the alien air. It tasted metallic, like copper and rain.

He was alive. But for how long?
